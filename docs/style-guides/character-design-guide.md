# Whisperwood Character Design Guide

> **Authority:** This document is the **highest authority for visual character creation** in the Whisperwood Universe. When visual, design, or illustration guidance conflicts with any other style note, prompt, or production brief — except [CANON.md](../../CANON.md) — **this guide prevails.**

> **Scope:** All characters — human, animal, and vehicle — across books, animation, games, apps, merchandise, marketing, and AI-generated media.

---

## Purpose

Whisperwood is built to last. Characters will appear across hundreds of stories, dozens of formats, and many years of production. Without a unified visual language, each new illustrator, animator, model, or AI system will interpret the world differently — and the universe will slowly fracture.

A **Character Design Guide** exists to prevent that drift.

This document defines the shared visual and creative language for every Whisperwood character. It ensures that:

- A child recognizes a Whisperwood character instantly, in any medium
- New characters feel like they belong beside existing ones
- Emotional expression remains safe, readable, and age-appropriate
- Production teams — human and automated — work from the same reference
- Design choices support storytelling values: kindness, curiosity, hope, and warmth

This guide is **not** a character profile. Individual characters are defined in [characters/](../../characters/). This guide defines the **rules of the visual world** those characters inhabit.

---

## Target Audience

**Primary audience:** Children aged **4–8**.

Every design decision must serve young viewers and readers who are still developing emotional literacy, visual attention, and trust in the stories they consume.

Whisperwood character designs must be:

| Quality | Meaning |
|---------|---------|
| **Friendly** | Approachable at first glance — no character should feel cold, hostile, or unknowable |
| **Instantly recognizable** | Strong silhouette, clear shapes, memorable proportions — identifiable in thumbnail or at a distance |
| **Emotionally expressive** | Feelings readable in face and body without dialogue or caption |
| **Safe** | Never frightening, threatening, or visually overwhelming |
| **Timeless** | Free of trend-driven fashion, dated styling, or reference humor that expires |

If a design works for adults but unsettles a five-year-old, it does not belong in Whisperwood.

## Character Philosophy

Whisperwood characters are **people you want to adventure with** — not figures to admire from a distance, and never figures to fear.

### Characters should feel

| Quality | Design expression |
|---------|-------------------|
| **Kind** | Open posture, soft edges, warm default expression, no aggressive default stance |
| **Curious** | Alert eyes, forward-leaning energy, attentive brow — interest before judgment |
| **Approachable** | Inviting proportions, readable face, no visual barriers ( spikes, harsh shadows, hidden eyes ) |
| **Imperfect** | Slight asymmetry in charm, relatable proportions — not fashion-model idealization |
| **Optimistic** | Light in the eyes, upward energy in pose — even worry reads as temporary |

Personality lives in **shape, pose, and expression** before color or costume detail. A character's design should communicate who they are when standing still.

### Avoid

| Avoid | Reason |
|-------|--------|
| **Aggressive appearance** | Sharp default poses, clenched fists, scowling resting face — violates safety and kindness |
| **Realistic anatomy** | Whisperwood is stylized storybook design, not anatomical study |
| **Scary proportions** | Oversized mouths, tiny eyes, elongated limbs, distorted features |
| **Hyper-detailed clothing** | Detail that cannot animate, reproduce, or remain consistent across media |
| **Complex accessories** | Visual noise that competes with face, silhouette, and signature item |

Characters may experience conflict, worry, or frustration in stories — but their **baseline design** must always feel trustworthy.

## Visual Style

Whisperwood characters belong to a **soft storybook illustration tradition** — warm, painterly or hand-drawn in feel, gentle in lighting, and clear in silhouette. The style favors emotional clarity over technical spectacle.

Think: *a beloved picture book brought to life* — not a blockbuster action aesthetic, not photorealism, not edgy minimalism.

### Overall characteristics

- Rounded forms with selective structure
- Warm daylight or gentle indoor lighting — rarely harsh contrast
- Visible brush or line quality welcome — perfection is not the goal; warmth is
- Eye-level framing for human and animal characters — the audience meets them as equals
- Backgrounds may be rich; **characters stay simple and readable**

### Head proportions

- **Children:** Head roughly **1:3 to 1:3.5** of total body height — larger heads increase expressiveness and youth read
- **Adults / mentors:** Head roughly **1:4 to 1:5** — still stylized, never realistic
- **Animals:** Head sized for expression first, species recognition second — expressive over accurate
- Cheeks may have soft fullness; jawlines stay gentle, never angular or severe

### Eyes

Eyes are the **primary emotional instrument**.

- Large relative to face — especially for child characters
- Clear iris and highlight — life and warmth; avoid dead or flat eyes
- Pupils soft, never pinprick or fully dilated in frightening ways
- Eye shape simple — readable at small sizes
- Brow supports emotion but never dominates the face into anger by default

**Avoid:** hollow eyes, sharp triangular eyes, heavy permanent shadows under eyes, realistic eye anatomy with visible veins or wetness.

### Smile

The default expression should trend **warm and open**.

- Simple mouth shapes — curved lines, soft openings
- Teeth visible only when appropriate to expression — never a fixed grin
- Smile reaches the eyes — a mouth-only smile feels hollow in Whisperwood
- Neutral and thinking expressions are valid defaults — not every character smiles constantly

### Hands

- **Simplified** — three or four fingers acceptable in stylized production; five when clarity allows
- Rounded knuckles, soft shapes — no detailed veins or wrinkled realism
- Gestures readable at distance — pointing, waving, holding objects clearly silhouetted
- Hands express emotion — open palms read as honesty; hidden hands reduce trust

### Body proportions

- **Child characters:** Compact torso, shorter limbs, energetic stance
- **Adult characters:** Taller, longer limbs, calmer vertical presence — still rounded, never heroic-realistic
- **Animal characters:** Slightly softened anatomy — real species cues, storybook proportions
- **Vehicle characters:** Rounded body panels, expressive front face or window "eyes" when personified

No muscle definition, no anatomical pin-up styling, no elongated fashion-proportion legs.

### Silhouette

Every canonical character must have a **distinct silhouette** recognizable in solid black.

Before color, ask:

> Could this character be identified by shadow alone?

Silhouette distinction comes from:

- Head shape and hair mass
- One signature item (backpack, hat, scarf, etc.)
- Posture tendency (lean forward = curious; wide stance = stable)
- Species or vehicle profile for non-human characters

If two characters share a silhouette, revise until they do not.

### Clothing simplicity

- **One or two dominant garment shapes** per character — shirt and pants, dress, overalls, simple coat
- Large color blocks, minimal pattern — patterns must survive shrink to icon size
- No logos, text, or real-world brands
- Clothing supports role (explorer, helper, homebody) without over-specifying era or trend

See [Clothing](#clothing) for extended guidance.

### Color harmony

Each character carries a **limited palette** — typically one primary body/clothing color, one secondary, and one accent. Palettes must harmonize with the Whisperwood world palette (earth tones, warm naturals, soft sky and forest hues).

Character colors should not clash with the guide's [Color Philosophy](#color-philosophy). When in doubt, soften and warm.

## Shape Language

Shapes communicate personality before a character speaks. Whisperwood uses a deliberate **soft geometry** system.

### Rounded shapes → Gentle · Safe · Friendly

- Circles and ovals dominate child characters, companions, and approachable mentors
- Rounded cheeks, soft hair masses, circular buttons, curved hems
- Default for protagonists and characters the audience should trust immediately

**Use for:** Main characters, young children, friendly animals, inviting vehicles.

### Square shapes → Reliable · Strong · Stable

- Rectangles and softened squares suggest sturdiness — blocky shoes, square packs, structured hats
- Corners are **never sharp** — always slightly rounded ("squircle" logic)
- Square language supports helpers, guardians, craftspeople, and calm mentors

**Use for:** Mentors, builders, protectors, steady friends — not for villains (Whisperwood has none designed to frighten).

### Triangular shapes → Use sparingly

- Triangles suggest energy, direction, and alertness — hat peaks, hair tufts, ear tips, roof lines on vehicles
- **Never frightening** — no sharp fangs, claw triangles, aggressive shoulder points, or villain horns
- Acute angles should feel **playful or attentive**, not dangerous

**Use for:** Accent only — a curious hair tuft, an explorer's cap, a cat's ear. Never as the dominant body form.

### Shape hierarchy per character

| Priority | Shape role |
|----------|------------|
| **Primary** | Rounded — establishes trust |
| **Secondary** | Square — optional stability cue |
| **Accent** | Triangle — energy or direction, minimal |

Every character should read **safe at a glance**. If shape language says "danger," redesign.

## Color Philosophy

Color carries emotion in Whisperwood. It is never decorative alone — it supports recognition, accessibility, and mood.

### Primary colors

Each character has **one dominant color identity** — the hue first seen in silhouette or thumbnail.

- Warm earth tones, soft greens, sky blues, gentle reds, honey yellows, bark browns
- Primary color appears on the largest garment or body surface
- Primary colors should feel **natural and grounded** — borrowed from forest, field, sky, and home

Avoid neon, hyper-saturated primaries, and gray-dominant palettes that read as cold or dystopian.

### Accent colors

**One accent** supports the primary — collar, scarf edge, backpack strap, cheek blush, hat band.

- Accent provides contrast for readability, not competition
- Accents repeat across a character's appearances — consistency builds recognition

### Contrast

- Sufficient contrast between character and background for readability in animation and print
- Sufficient contrast between facial features (eyes, mouth) and skin/fur for expressiveness
- Avoid placing dark characters against dark backgrounds without a light rim or setting adjustment

### Accessibility

- Do not rely on color alone to convey essential information — shape and label support color
- Test designs in grayscale — silhouette and expression must remain clear
- Consider color-blind safe pairing — avoid red-green as the only distinction between two main characters

### Consistency

- Canonical character colors are **fixed** once approved — record in `profile.json` → `appearance.colors`
- Seasonal or story-driven color changes require editorial approval
- Merchandise, AI generation, and animation must reference the same approved palette

### Emotional meaning

| Color family | Whisperwood association |
|--------------|-------------------------|
| **Warm yellow / honey** | Joy, optimism, curiosity |
| **Soft green** | Nature, growth, calm adventure |
| **Sky blue** | Openness, clarity, friendship |
| **Earth brown / bark** | Groundedness, reliability, home |
| **Gentle red / terracotta** | Warmth, enthusiasm — never aggression |
| **Soft purple / lavender** | Wonder, imagination — use lightly |
| **Cream / warm white** | Safety, comfort, light |

Color supports story — it does not stereotype character roles.

## Expressions

Whisperwood characters express emotion **clearly and safely**. The audience aged 4–8 must read feeling in the face without confusion or fear.

### Standard emotional range

| Expression | Visual read | Notes |
|------------|-------------|-------|
| **Happy** | Soft smile, bright eyes, relaxed brow | Warmth without manic energy |
| **Curious** | Slight lean, raised brow, focused eyes, mouth slightly open | Signature Whisperwood look — wonder is welcome |
| **Thinking** | Eyes up or aside, gentle brow pinch, still mouth | Puzzle-solving, not worry |
| **Excited** | Wide eyes, open smile, elevated posture | Joyful — never aggressive or out of control |
| **Surprised** | Raised brows, open mouth (soft O), widened eyes | Mild surprise only — never horror |
| **Worried** | Brows inward-soft (not angry), smaller mouth, attentive eyes | Concern is honest; always resolves toward hope in story |
| **Proud** | Lifted chin gently, satisfied smile, bright eyes | Quiet accomplishment — not smug |
| **Sleepy** | Half-closed lids, relaxed mouth, softened posture | Calm, never distressed |
| **Laughing** | Closed or crescent eyes, open joyful mouth | Full delight — never mockery of others |
| **Crying** | Tears visible but gentle, soft brow, no distortion | Sadness is valid; never grotesque or manipulative |

### Expression principles

- **Extremes are soft.** Even big joy or sadness stays within storybook readability.
- **No frightening faces.** No sharp teeth, rage, horror eyes, or realistic crying distortion.
- **Emotions resolve.** Story context may show worry or sadness; design must support return to safety.
- **Whole-face acting.** Brow, eyes, mouth, and cheek work together — not mouth-only animation.
- **Consistent model.** Each character's face structure stays fixed; expressions deform gently within that model.

### Expression sheet requirement

Canonical characters should maintain an **approved expression sheet** in `assets/images/` before full production. Minimum set: happy, curious, thinking, worried, laughing.

## Clothing

Clothing communicates role without locking design to a year, region, or trend.

### Principles

| Principle | Guidance |
|-----------|----------|
| **Simple** | One or two garments dominate; avoid layered complexity |
| **Recognizable** | Silhouette distinct at icon size — overalls read as overalls, cloak as cloak |
| **Easy to animate** | Few loose parts; no excessive frills, straps, or dangling elements unless signature |
| **Easy for AI image generation** | Large color fields, clear garment names, minimal micro-pattern |
| **No tiny decorative details** | No lace grids, micro-prints, embroidery filigree, or jewelry clusters |

### Garment guidance

- **Tops:** Simple shirts, tunics, sweaters — one collar type, one sleeve length per character
- **Bottoms:** Pants, shorts, skirts, overalls — clear shape, minimal pockets or seams drawn
- **Outerwear:** One optional layer (coat, vest, poncho) — used consistently or seasonally with approval
- **Footwear:** Simple shoes or boots — rounded, no brand detail
- **Headwear:** Optional — if present, often becomes part of signature silhouette

### What to avoid

- Real-world fashion trends (streetwear drops, meme clothing, licensed sports kits)
- Text, logos, or numbers on clothing
- Over-specified cultural costumes without editorial research and approval
- Armor, weapons, or military styling
- Clothing that sexualizes or ages up child characters

### Flexibility clause

Individual character profiles may keep clothing **generically described** (e.g. "practical adventure wear") to allow artistic evolution — but **silhouette, palette, and signature item remain fixed**. See [characters/](../../characters/) and [character.schema.json](../../schemas/character.schema.json) → `appearance`.

## Signature Items

Important characters should carry **one memorable item** — a single object that anchors recognition across media.

### Why one item

- **Instant recognition** at thumbnail, toy shelf, and app icon scale
- **Story continuity** — the item travels with the character through adventures
- **Production efficiency** — animators, illustrators, and AI systems have a fixed visual anchor
- **Merchandise clarity** — one hero prop per character simplifies product design

### Strong signature item examples

| Item | Narrative feel |
|------|----------------|
| **Backpack** | Explorer, preparedness, journey |
| **Lantern** | Guidance, warmth, finding the way |
| **Scarf** | Comfort, home, seasonal warmth |
| **Hat** | Identity, role, playful distinction |
| **Notebook** | Observer, recorder, curiosity |

Signature items are defined in `profile.json` → `signatureItems` and may link to [objects/](../../objects/) records when created.

### Rules

- **One primary signature item** per flagship character — optional secondary only with approval
- Item must appear in **most canonical illustrations** (`alwaysPresent: true` in JSON)
- Item design is **simple** — readable shape, limited color, no moving parts unless essential
- **Never overload** with accessories — no hats plus scarves plus bags plus tools plus jewelry on equal visual weight

If everything is special, nothing is recognizable.

## Animation Principles

Whisperwood characters move with **storybook physics** — soft, playful, expressive, and readable. They do not move like realistic humans or action heroes.

### Core movement qualities

| Quality | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Soft** | Ease-in and ease-out on all motion; no snappy mechanical starts unless comic and gentle |
| **Playful** | Bounce in step, slight overshoot on jumps, alive idle — characters breathe and shift |
| **Expressive** | Whole-body acting — shoulders, hips, and hands participate in emotion |
| **Readable** | Poses clear at a glance; silhouette maintained during motion |
| **Never overly realistic** | No mocap-heavy weight, athletic precision, or fight choreography |

### Movement by character type

- **Children:** Energetic but controlled — quick steps, small jumps, forward lean when curious
- **Mentors:** Slower, grounded movement — fewer gestures, more deliberate
- **Animals:** Species-appropriate but softened — bounce, wiggle, flap — never feral or scary
- **Vehicles:** Gentle acceleration, slight squash on stops, expressive headlight or window "face"

### Standard clip expectations

Align with `animationProfile.clips` in character JSON — idle, walk, run, jump, wave, laugh, thinking, celebrate at minimum where applicable.

### Animation don'ts

- Hyper-realistic walk cycles with weight shift detail
- Violent impacts, combat dodges, or slapstick injury
- Fast chaotic camera paired with frantic un readable motion
- Frozen mannequin idle — characters must feel alive in stillness

Structured per-character direction: `profile.json` → `animationProfile` and `profile.md` → Animation Notes.

## AI Generation Guidelines

AI tools will illustrate, animate, and extend Whisperwood across books, videos, games, and merchandise. Without rules, each generation drifts.

### Authority order for AI visual work

1. [CANON.md](../../CANON.md) — content boundaries and world rules
2. **This guide** — visual language and design standards
3. [characters/{id}/profile.json](../../characters/) — character-specific facts, colors, profiles
4. [characters/{id}/profile.md](../../characters/) — expanded creative notes

When JSON and this guide align, both apply. When conflict appears, escalate to editorial review — do not guess.

### Preserve consistency across media

| Medium | Primary consistency anchors |
|--------|----------------------------|
| **Images** | Silhouette, palette, expression model, signature item, `imageProfile` |
| **Videos / animation** | Movement principles, expression range, clip references in `animationProfile` |
| **Books** | Same character model across pages — no drift in hair, clothing, or proportions |
| **Games** | Readable silhouette at small scale; simplified geometry matching design guide |
| **Merchandise** | Signature item and primary color locked; shapes simplified but recognizable |

### Using character `imageProfile`

Each canonical character may define:

- `defaultPrompt` — merge with this guide's language, never replace it
- `negativePrompt` — extend with guide prohibitions (realistic, scary, detailed clothing, logos)
- `artStyle`, `lighting`, `renderingStyle`, `cameraStyle` — must harmonize with [Visual Style](#visual-style)

**Do not reference specific AI models, engines, or proprietary tool settings** in canonical records. Describe visual outcomes only.

### AI assistant checklist (before generating)

- [ ] Read this guide and the character's `profile.json` + `profile.md`
- [ ] Use approved palette and signature item
- [ ] Maintain silhouette and proportions for character age/type
- [ ] Keep expression within [standard emotional range](#expressions)
- [ ] Apply soft storybook style — warm light, simple clothing, rounded shapes
- [ ] Exclude: horror, realism, aggression, hyper-detail, brands, weapons, frightening staging
- [ ] Validate output against silhouette test (recognizable in grayscale?)

### Batch and variant generation

When generating multiple images of one character:

- Fix **one reference sheet** as source of truth before volume production
- Variations may change **pose, expression, and setting** — not face structure, palette, or signature item
- "Redesign" or "reimagine" prompts are **not permitted** for canonical characters without approval

## Long-term Scalability

Whisperwood is designed to grow — more characters, more episodes, more formats, more regions of production. This guide is the **immune system** against visual drift.

### How guidelines scale

| Challenge | How this guide helps |
|-----------|---------------------|
| **100+ characters** | Shared shape language, palette rules, and silhouette discipline keep newcomers compatible |
| **Multiple illustrators** | One handbook replaces oral tradition — onboarding is document-driven |
| **Multiple countries / languages** | Visual language is universal; expression and shape transcend translation |
| **New media formats** | Simplified clothing and strong silhouettes survive downscale to icons and up-scale to print |
| **AI model updates** | Outcome-based rules (warm, soft, readable) survive tool changes better than tool-specific prompts |
| **Merchandise and licensing** | Signature item doctrine and fixed palettes simplify product lines |

### Character onboarding pipeline

When creating a new canonical character:

1. Design against this guide — not against a single favorite illustration
2. Validate silhouette, palette, expression sheet, and signature item
3. Record structured data in `profile.json` → `appearance`, `signatureItems`, `imageProfile`, `animationProfile`
4. Write expanded notes in `profile.md` → Visual Notes
5. Submit for editorial review before `status: canonical`

### Revision policy

Changes to this guide affect **every character** and **every format**. Revisions require intentional authorship — same standard as [CANON.md](../../CANON.md).

Individual characters evolve in **detail refinement**, not **identity overhaul**. Zulk today should read as Zulk in ten years.

### Success metric

> A child who has not seen Whisperwood in months should still recognize a character instantly — and feel welcome.

That is the standard this guide exists to protect.
