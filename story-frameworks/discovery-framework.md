# Discovery Framework

> **Framework ID:** `discovery`  
> **Primary theme:** Curiosity, wonder, and finding something new  
> **Audience:** Ages 4–8  
> **Status:** Editorial blueprint — not a canon story

---

## Purpose

The Discovery Framework structures stories driven by **curiosity** — characters who notice, question, explore, and find something they did not expect. The central reward is not treasure or victory but **understanding**: a new place, creature, idea, pattern, or truth about the world or themselves.

This framework serves Whisperwood's conviction that **curiosity leads to discovery** and that paying attention is an act of courage. It is the natural home for explorer-shaped characters and stories that leave children wanting to look closer at their own world.

Use this framework when the story's engine is *I wonder what that is?* — not when the primary stake is friendship repair or helping someone in distress (though discovery stories may include those as secondary threads).

---

## Target Emotions

| Phase | Emotions |
|-------|----------|
| **Opening** | Calm interest, alertness, pleasant anticipation |
| **Conflict** | Frustration, uncertainty, the itch of an unanswered question |
| **Escalation** | Determination, growing wonder, occasional gentle setback |
| **Resolution** | Delight, satisfaction, awe — quiet or bright |
| **Closing** | Expanded worldview, eagerness to discover more |

Discovery stories should feel **bright and open**. Even setbacks are interesting, not frightening. The unknown is inviting, not threatening.

---

## Typical Beginning

- A character notices something unusual — a sound, track, color, shape, or gap in their knowledge
- The setting invites exploration: edge of a familiar place, new season, unfamiliar path, or object out of place
- A question is asked aloud or shown through action — leaning in, pointing, pausing
- Companions may share or differ in enthusiasm; curiosity is the shared fuel

The beginning hooks with **attention**, not action. Something is worth looking at.

---

## Typical Conflict

The conflict is **intellectual and exploratory**, not adversarial:

- The answer is not immediately visible — the discovery requires patience or a different approach
- A wrong assumption leads down an unhelpful path
- Nature or circumstance slows progress — weather, distance, time of day, or a shy creature
- A companion disagrees on what is important to investigate
- The discoverer must choose between rushing ahead and observing carefully

There is no villain. The "opponent" is **not knowing yet** — and that is a worthy challenge.

---

## Escalation

Escalation deepens the search and raises the quality of attention:

- Clues accumulate — footprints, sounds, patterns, partial answers
- Characters try multiple approaches: looking closer, asking an animal friend, retracing steps, waiting quietly
- A moment of doubt — maybe the answer will not come today
- Sensory richness increases — the world feels more alive as characters pay attention
- The discovery feels **near** but not yet reached — tension of anticipation, not fear

Escalation should model **persistence without recklessness**. Characters try again, but they do not ignore safety or kindness to force an answer.

---

## Resolution

The discovery arrives through **observation, patience, and cooperation**:

- The answer reveals itself to careful attention — not to force
- Characters share the moment of finding — pointing, naming, wondering together
- The discovery may be small (a beetle, a seed, a hidden path) or meaningful (a place, a truth, a new friend) — but it always feels **earned**
- Characters reflect briefly — not a lecture, but a line of wonder or gratitude
- The story closes with the world feeling slightly larger than before

Magic, if present, is **gentle** — it may highlight what was already there, not replace effort and curiosity.

---

## Lesson

> **Curiosity opens the world.** Looking closely, asking questions, and waiting patiently reveal wonders that rushing would miss.

Secondary lessons may include:

- Wrong guesses are part of learning
- Sharing a discovery doubles the joy
- Nature rewards respect and patience
- Not every question is answered today — and that is okay

The lesson must arrive through **the act of discovering**, not through a character explaining it to the audience.

## Suitable Character Roles

| Role | Function in this framework |
|------|---------------------------|
| **Explorer** | Primary driver — asks questions, leads attention, accepts wrong turns |
| **Main / Protagonist** | Owns the emotional journey of wonder |
| **Observer** | Notices details others skip; may hold the key clue |
| **Helper** | Supports the search — carries tools, encourages patience |
| **Mentor** | Models how to look without taking over the discovery |
| **Comic Relief** | Adds lightness during frustration; never mocks curiosity |
| **Animal character** | May hold knowledge, react to nature, or guide gently |

At least one character must embody **genuine curiosity**. Discovery stories fail when a passive character is dragged through the plot without wondering.

---

## Suitable Location Types

Locations should reward **attention** — rich in sensory detail and hidden layers:

| Location type | Why it works |
|---------------|--------------|
| **Forest / woodland** | Layers of life, sounds, paths, and secrets |
| **Meadow / field** | Open visibility with small hidden details |
| **Stream / pond / wetland** | Reflection, movement, life at the water's edge |
| **Garden** | Cultivated wonder — growth, insects, seasons |
| **Trail / path edge** | Boundary between known and unknown |
| **Rocky outcrop / hilltop** | Vista and perspective — seeing farther |
| **Seasonal landscape** | Change as discovery — autumn leaves, spring buds |
| **Attic / curiosity corner (indoor)** | Safe indoor discovery of forgotten objects |

Avoid locations that overwhelm or frighten. Discovery settings are **inviting**, even when unfamiliar.

---

## Suitable Object Types

Objects support investigation and reward attention:

| Object type | Narrative use |
|-------------|---------------|
| **Magnifying tool / viewing aid** | Looking closer — metaphor for attention |
| **Collection container** | Gathering specimens or treasures respectfully |
| **Map or journal** | Recording what is found; planning next steps |
| **Explorer pack / bag** | Preparedness for curiosity |
| **Natural specimen** | Leaf, feather, stone — the discovery itself |
| **Compass or direction tool** | Finding the way when paths branch |
| **Light source (lantern, etc.)** | Revealing what was hidden in dim light — gently |

Objects enable discovery; they do not **replace** the character's effort to notice and understand.

---

## Typical Episode Duration

| Format | Duration |
|--------|----------|
| **Read-aloud / picture book** | 6–9 minutes |
| **Animated short** | 8–11 minutes |
| **Interactive app experience** | 7–12 minutes |
| **Story outline / beat sheet** | 10–14 story beats |

Discovery stories need room for **slow looking** — beats where characters pause, listen, and observe. Do not compress the search into a montage of instant answers.

---

## Typical Age Suitability

**Primary:** Ages 4–8

| Age band | Adaptation notes |
|----------|------------------|
| **4–5** | One clear question, one clear answer; heavy sensory description |
| **6–7** | A false lead or wrong guess before the true discovery |
| **7–8** | Slightly layered discovery — surface find leads to deeper understanding |

All variants must treat nature with respect and keep wonder ahead of exposition.

---

## Common Storytelling Mistakes

| Mistake | Why it fails | Corrective guidance |
|---------|--------------|---------------------|
| **Discovery is handed over by magic alone** | Undermines curiosity and effort | Magic may reveal; characters must still engage |
| **The find is too grandiose** | Breaks gentle scale of Whisperwood | Prefer small, meaningful discoveries |
| **Characters do not wonder aloud or act curious** | Breaks framework purpose | Show questions, pauses, and wrong guesses |
| **Nature is treated as a prop** | Violates respect-for-nature values | Living things have agency and dignity |
| **Frustration becomes anger or meanness** | Wrong emotional register | Frustration leads to trying differently |
| **Info-dump at the end** | Preaching replaces wonder | Let the find speak; one line of awe is enough |
| **No sensory detail** | Discovery feels abstract | Use sound, texture, color, movement |
| **Danger added for excitement** | Violates child safety rules | Tension is anticipation, not peril |

## AI Generation Guidance

When generating a story from this framework:

1. **Define the central question** — What does the character want to know or find?
2. **Cast an explorer or curious protagonist** from canonical records
3. **Choose a location rich in sensory detail** — match location `storyRoles` to exploration
4. **Plan at least one wrong turn** — a guess that teaches patience
5. **Allocate beats to observation** — not every beat advances plot; some deepen attention
6. **Resolve through character action** — looking, listening, waiting, asking
7. **Close with expanded wonder** — the world feels bigger, not "finished"
8. **Validate against CANON** — gentle magic only, nature respected, no horror

### Prompt scaffolding (internal)

```
Framework: discovery
Central question: [what the character wants to understand]
Discovery type: [place | creature | object | pattern | idea]
False lead: [optional wrong guess]
Resolution mechanism: [observation | patience | cooperation | respectful interaction]
Lesson (implicit): curiosity and attention reveal wonder
```

Do not name the discovery in generic terms that become accidental canon. Tie specifics to approved locations, objects, and characters at generation time.
